An American reckoning

End poverty and create a
steady floor for a changing economy

35.9 million Americans live in poverty in the world's wealthiest nation. Workers' wages have lagged behind productivity for 50 years. Automation is accelerating. Universal Basic Income is the most direct response to all three.

01

The first argument

America has the wealth to end poverty. Right now.

The US has a $28 trillion economy — the largest in human history. Filling the entire national poverty gap, lifting every person above the poverty line, would cost an estimated $288 billion a year. Federal Safety Net

That's roughly 1% of GDP — less than the annual defense budget, less than what Americans spend on lottery tickets and tobacco combined. Yet in 2024, 35.9 million Americans still lived below the poverty line. US Census 2024

Poverty in America is not a resource problem. It is a policy choice. UBI is the most direct way to undo it — cash in people's hands, not routed through a bureaucratic maze of programs that fail to reach millions who need them.

02

The second argument

Workers are owed money. The math says so.

From 1948 to the early 1970s, when American workers became more productive, their wages rose to match. It was a deal: work harder, earn more, share in the country's growth.

Then the deal broke. Since 1979, productivity has grown nearly 3× faster than typical worker pay. The economy kept growing. Workers kept showing up. The gains went somewhere else — to shareholders, executives, and capital owners. Economic Policy Institute

That gap represents decades of labor value that workers created but never received. A UBI isn't charity — it's a partial correction of a decades-long transfer of wealth from workers to capital.

03

The third argument

Automation is coming. Retraining programs don't work.

AI and robotics aren't just changing work — they are eliminating entire job categories faster than new ones appear. McKinsey estimates up to 400 million jobs could be displaced globally by 2030. In the US, researchers at Oxford estimated 47% of all jobs are at high risk. McKinsey

The standard political response is "job retraining programs." But government retraining programs have a dismal track record — most displaced workers do not successfully transition to new careers through them. And those who do still face the problem of who pays the bills during the transition.

A UBI doesn't just soften the blow of automation. It gives displaced workers the time, security, and dignity to figure out what comes next — whether that's retraining, starting a business, or caring for a family member while the economy catches up.

There's also an overlooked flip side: automation creates extraordinary wealth. A UBI can function as a citizen's dividend on the automation economy — a share of the gains distributed to the public who made it possible.

04

The fourth argument

A floor gives workers the power to say no — and to build something of their own.

Right now, 60% of Americans can't cover a $1,000 emergency from savings. Federal Reserve That is not just a personal hardship — it is a structural power imbalance. When you can't afford a month without a paycheck, you can't walk away from a bad boss, a toxic workplace, or a job that's going nowhere. Employers know this.

A guaranteed income floor changes that calculation entirely. It gives every American worker genuine leverage — the ability to say no, to take their time, to find work worth doing rather than accept whatever's available out of desperation.

And it doesn't stop at walking away. The countries with the strongest social floors — Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands — have higher rates of entrepreneurship than the US. OECD A UBI is a startup fund for the bottom half of the country. It's the cushion that lets a warehouse worker in Ohio try the business idea they've had for ten years.

Today, 1 in 4 American workers held some form of gig or contract work in 2024. ADP Research Most lack health insurance, retirement savings, or unemployment protection. A UBI provides the floor that makes precarious work survivable while we build a better system.

The idea

So what exactly is Universal Basic Income?

UBI is a regular cash payment to every adult citizen — no means-testing, no work requirements, no strings attached. The same amount whether you're a CEO, a caregiver, or between jobs. It's a floor, not a ceiling.

It's a 400-year-old idea with supporters spanning the entire political spectrum — from libertarian economists who want to replace the welfare bureaucracy, to labor leaders who want to recognize unpaid work, to tech founders who see it as the answer to AI job displacement.

Real-world proof

It's not theory. It's already working.

Dozens of controlled experiments across the US and the world have tested basic income with real people and real money. Here are three of the most significant.

🇺🇸

Alaska Permanent Fund

Every Alaskan has received an annual oil dividend since 1982 — no application, no conditions. The longest-running basic income program in the world.

15–25k residents kept out of poverty each year ISER
🇫🇮

Finland Pilot

2,000 unemployed citizens received €560/month for two years with no conditions. A government-run, peer-reviewed randomized trial.

↑ Wellbeing less stress, more trust, equal employment Finnish Ministry
🇺🇸

OpenResearch Study

1,000 low-income Americans received $1,000/month for 3 years. The largest randomized UBI trial in US history, funded by Sam Altman.

+$169/mo more on food, rent & transport OpenResearch

Common objections

Myth vs. reality

UBI attracts strong reactions. Here are the most common objections and what the evidence actually says.

"People will just stop working."+
Reality: In Stockton, full-time employment among recipients doubled from 28% to 40%. SEED In Finland, work rates were nearly identical to the control group. Finnish Ministry Most people continue working for purpose, social connection, and additional income.
"It would cause runaway inflation."+
Reality: Inflation depends on how UBI is funded. If redistributed from higher earners, no new money enters the economy. Most serious proposals use tax reform, not money printing. Roosevelt Inst.
"We can't afford it — it would cost trillions."+
Reality: The gross cost is not the net cost. Most proposals replace existing welfare programs and are funded through new taxes. A Roosevelt Institute analysis found a $1,000/month UBI funded by a VAT could be roughly revenue-neutral. Roosevelt Institute
"People will spend it on drugs and alcohol."+
Reality: In Stockton, top spending categories were food, utilities, and car repairs. A World Bank meta-analysis of 19 countries found the average effect on alcohol and tobacco spending was slightly negative. Evans & Popova
"It's a socialist policy."+
Reality: Milton Friedman (conservative economist) advocated a negative income tax. Friedman Friedrich Hayek supported a minimum income floor. UBI has genuine libertarian credentials — it replaces bureaucracy with a direct cash transfer.
